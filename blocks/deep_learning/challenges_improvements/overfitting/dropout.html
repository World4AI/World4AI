Dropout

Dropout is a regularization technique was developed by Geoffrey Hinton and his colleagues at the university of Toronto [1] [2] . The idea seems so simple, that it is almost preposterous that it works at all.

At trainig time at each training step with a probability of p undefined a neuron is deactivated, its value is set to 0. You can start the interactive example and observe how it looks like with a p undefined value of 0.2.

When we use our model for inference, we do not remove any of the nodes. If we did that, we would get different results each time we run a model. By not deactivating any nodes we introduce a problem though. Because more neurons are active, each layer has to deal with an input that is on a different scale, than the one the neural network has seen during training. Different conditions during training and inference will prevent the neural network from generating good predictions. To prevent that from happening the active nodes are scaled by \dfrac{1}{1-p} undefined during training. We skip that scaling during inference and thus create similar conditions for training and inference.

Let us assume for example that the activation layer contains only 1's and p undefined is 0.5.

\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{bmatrix} undefined

The dropout layer will zero out roughly half of the activations and multiply the rest by \dfrac{1}{1-0.5} = 2 undefined.

\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 2 \\ 0 \\ 2 \\ \end{bmatrix} undefined

But why is the dropout procedure helpful in avoiding overfitting? Each time we remove a set of activations from training, we essentially create a different model. This simplified model has to learn to deal with the task at hand without overrelying on any of the previous activations, because any of those might get deactivated at any time. The final model can be seen as an ensemble of an immensely huge collection of simplified models. Ensembles models tend to produce better results and reduce overfitting. You will notice that in practice dropout works extremely well.
