Gradient Descent

Cross-Entropy vs Mean Squared Error

The cross-entropy is almost exclusively used as the loss function for classification tasks, but it is not obvious why we can not use the mean squared error. Actually we can, but as we will see shortly, the cross-entropy is a more convenient measure of loss for classification tasks.

For this discusson we will deal with a single sample and distinquish between two cases: the label y undefined is either 0 or 1. If the label equals to 0, the mean squared error is (0 - \sigma)^2 undefined while the cross-entropy is-\log(1 - \sigma) undefined. Both losses increase as the predicted probability \sigma undefined grows. If the true label 1 undefined is 1 on the other hand the mean squared error is (1 - \sigma)^2 undefined and the cross-entropy is-\log(\sigma) undefined. In both cases the error decreases when the predicted probability \sigma undefined grows.

Below we plot the mean squared error and the cross-entropy based on the predicted probability \sigma undefined. The red plot depicts the mean squared error, while the blue plot depicts the cross-entropy. There are two plots for each of the losses, one for each value of the target.

The mean squared error and the cross-entropy start at the same position, but the difference in errors starts to grow as the predicted probability starts to deviate from the true label. The cross-entropy punishes misclassifications with a much higher loss, than the mean squared error. When we deal with probabilities the difference between the label and the predicted probability can not be larger than 1. That means that the mean squared error also can not grow beyond 1. The logarithm on the other hand literally explodes when the value starts approaching 0.

This behaviour can also be observed when we draw the predicted probability against the derivative of the loss function. While the derivatives of the mean squared error are linear, the cross-entropy derivatives grow exponentially when the quality of predictions deteriorates.

The exponential growth of derivatives implies, that the gradient descent algorithm will take much larger steps, when the classification predictions are way off, thereby converging at a higher rate.

The Algorithm

Our goal is to find weights \mathbf{w} undefined and the bias b undefined that minimize the cross-entropy loss in a binary classification problem.

\underset{\mathbf{w}, b} {\arg\min} L \\ L = - \dfrac{1}{n} \sum_i \Big[y^{(i)} \log \sigma + (1 - y^{(i)}) \log(1 - \sigma) \Big] \\ undefined

Similar to linear regression, logistic regression relies on gradient descent. While the derivative is slightly more complex, the chain rule still allows us to relatively easily find the optimum. Ultimately we are interested in the gradient of the loss function L undefined with respect to the weight vector \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} undefined and the derivative with respect to the bias \dfrac{\partial}{\partial b} L undefined. Once we have those we can use the same gradient descent procedure that we used in linear regression.

\mathbf{w}_{t+1} \coloneqq \mathbf{w}_t - \alpha \mathbf{\nabla}_w undefined
b_{t+1} \coloneqq b_t - \alpha \dfrac{\partial}{\partial b} undefined

We can calculate gradients for individual samples and calculate the mean afterwards. That reduces the loss of an individual sample to the following equation.

L = - \Big[y \log \sigma + (1 - y) \log(1 - \sigma)\Big] \\ undefined

We start by calculating the derivative of the loss with respect to the sigmoid output.

\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \sigma}L = - \Big[y^{(i)} \dfrac{1}{\sigma} - (1 - y^{(i)}) \dfrac{1}{(1 - \sigma)}\Big] \\ undefined

Next we calculate the derivative of the sigmoid \sigma(z) = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} undefined with respect to the net input z undefined.

\dfrac{\partial}{\partial z}\sigma = \sigma(z) (1 - \sigma(z)) undefined

The derivative of the sigmoid function is relatively straightforward, but the derivation process is somewhat mathematically involved. It is not necessary to know the exact steps how we arrive at the derivative, but if you are interested, below we provide the necessary steps.

\begin{aligned} \dfrac{\partial}{\partial z}\sigma &= \dfrac{\partial}{\partial z} \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \\ &= \dfrac{\partial}{\partial z} ({1 + e^{-z}})^{-1} \\ &= -({1 + e^{-z}})^{-2} * -e^{-z} \\ &= \dfrac{ e^{-z}}{({1 + e^{-z}})^{-2}} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \dfrac{ e^{-z}}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \dfrac{ 1 + e^{-z} - 1}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \\ &= \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \Big(\dfrac{ 1 + e^{-z}}{1 + e^{-z}} - \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}}\Big) \\ &= \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}} \Big(1 - \dfrac{1}{{1 + e^{-z}}}\Big) \\ &= \sigma(z) (1 - \sigma(z)) \end{aligned} undefined

The rest of derivations are do not differ from those that we used in linear regression. We calculate the gradients of the net input z = \mathbf{xw^T}+b undefined with respect to the individual weights and the bias applying the chain rule.

\dfrac{\partial}{\partial w_j} z = x_j undefined
\dfrac{\partial}{\partial b} z = 1 undefined

Finally we can utilize the chain rule to calculate the derivatives of the loss with respect to the weights and the bias. When we are dealing with batch or minibatch gradient descent we collect the gradients for several samples n undefined and calculate the mean, which is utilized in gradient descent.

\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w_j} = \dfrac{1}{n} \sum_i^n \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \sigma^{(i)}} \dfrac{\partial \sigma^{(i)}}{\partial z^{(i)}} \dfrac{\partial z^{(i)}}{\partial w_j^{(i)}} undefined
