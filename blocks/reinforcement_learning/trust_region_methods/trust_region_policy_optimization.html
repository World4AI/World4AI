Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO)

We will start our discussion of Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) with a slight reformulation of our objective.

J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta}}\Big[\sum_t \gamma^t R_t\Big] undefined

The objective we are usually trying to maximize is the expected sum of discounted rewards, as defined above. Now imagine we have two policies at our disposal : the new policy J(\theta') undefined that we are trying to optimize and some old policy J(\theta) undefined. We can define our objective as the performance difference between the two policies.

J(\theta') - J(\theta) undefined

Maximizing the distance between the two performance measures with respect to \theta' undefined is the same as optimizing J(\theta') undefined. That is because J(\theta) undefined is essentially a constant when we calculate a gradient with respect to \theta' undefined.

The actual distance between the two performance measure is \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta'}}\Big[\sum_t \gamma^t A^{\theta}\Big] undefined, where we use the advantage function calculated based on the old policy with parameters \theta undefined, but the expectation is calcualated based on the new policy with parameters \theta' undefined. When we chage a policy from \pi_{\theta} undefined to \pi_{\theta'} undefined we change the trajectories that the policy generates. Each of the new states and actions that form the trajectory produce advantages compared to the old policy and the sum of those advantages is the difference in policies. If we look closely we can additionally recognize, that as long as the advantages are positive the policy \pi_{\theta'} undefined is going to be an improvement over the policy \pi_{\theta} undefined.

J(\theta') - J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\theta'}}\Big[\sum_t \gamma^t A^{\theta}\Big] undefined

We can decompose the expectation above into two parts. The part that is dependent on the dynamics of the Markov Decision Process and the part that is dependent on the policy. The dynamics \rho_{\pi_{\theta'}}(s) undefined tell us how often in expectation does the agent land in state s undefined. The policy \pi_{\theta'} undefined is the mapping from states to actions according to the new policy.

J(\theta') - J(\theta) = \sum_s \rho_{\pi_{\theta'}}(s) \sum_a \pi_{\theta'}(a) \Big[A^{\theta}(s, a)\Big] undefined

There is a problem that we are facing now. We would like to use the data that we collected using the old policy \pi_{\theta} undefined to optimize our new policy \pi_{\theta'} undefined. But the dynamics \rho undefined and the policy \pi undefined need to be collected using the new parameters \theta' undefined. We can deal with the policy using importance sampling.

J(\theta') - J(\theta) = \sum_s \rho_{\pi_{\theta'}}(s) \sum_a \pi_{\theta}(a) \Big[\dfrac{\pi_{\theta'}}{\pi_{\theta}}A^{\theta}(s, a)\Big] undefined

On the other hand we have no way of dealing with \rho undefined, because that would require sampling with the new policy and our goal is to use samples from the old policy. The idea is to assume\rho_{\theta'} \approx \rho_{\theta} undefined. As it turns out this is only valid when the policy\pi_{\theta} undefined and\pi_{\theta'} undefined are close to each other. The authors of TRPO decided to use KL divergence as their measure of closeness, so that D_{KL}(\pi_{\theta'} || \pi_{\theta} ) < \epsilon undefined. That we define a "trust region", where the difference between the two distributions is close and it is safe to take a gradient ascent step.

\max_{\theta} {E}_{\theta} \Big[\dfrac{\pi_{\theta'}}{\pi_{\theta}}\hat{A}^{\theta}\Big] \text{, such that } D_{KL}(\pi_{\theta'} || \pi_{\theta} ) < \epsilon undefined

The optimization problem that we end up with looks as above. When \theta undefined and \theta' undefined are equal the KL divergence is 0 and the left hand side corresponds to an actor-critic optimization problem. When as in TRPO this is not the case the left side is called the "surrogate loss", because we approximate the loss function that requires sampling using the new policy through samples from the old policy. We do in order to use old samples several times before we discard them. Additionally the KL divergence creates a "trust region" that does not allow the new policy to stray away from the old policy. The whole process makes TRPO a more stable algorithm than the usual state of the art actor-critic methods.

We are not going to further discuss how the optimization problem can be solved and how the algorithm exactly looks like. This is because on the one hand the derivations in TRPO are quite involved and on the other hand TRPO was used as a stepping stone for PPO. PPO is a much easier and popular algorithm, that builds on the foundations that we have discussed so far. Unlike TRPO it can be implemented using the standard autodiff libraries without additional overhead and has been used to create state of the art results.
